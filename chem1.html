Separation Techniques

Mixtures are combinations of substances that are not chemically bonded. We can separate them using techniques that exploit differences in their physical properties. This section explores common methods used to purify substances from various types of mixtures.

Filtration

Used for: Separating an insoluble solid from a liquid (e.g., sand from water).

Principle: Difference in particle size. The filter paper has pores that allow liquid particles to pass through but trap the larger solid particles.

Evaporation

Used for: Separating a soluble solid from a liquid when the liquid is not needed (e.g., salt from water).

Principle: Difference in boiling points. The liquid is heated until it evaporates, leaving the solid behind.

Distillation

Used for: Separating two miscible liquids with different boiling points, or a soluble solid from a liquid when the liquid needs to be collected.

Principle: Difference in boiling points. The mixture is heated, the liquid with the lower boiling point vaporizes first, then it is cooled and condensed back into a liquid in a separate container.

Separating Funnel

Used for: Separating immiscible liquids (e.g., oil and water).

Principle: Difference in density. The liquids form distinct layers, and the denser liquid at the bottom can be drained off.

Percentage Composition

Formula: % by mass = (mass of component / total mass of mixture) x 100.

Example: A 20g sample of salt water contains 5g of salt. The percentage composition of salt is (5g / 20g) x 100 = 25%.

The Model of the Atom

Our understanding of the atom has evolved significantly over time as new experimental evidence emerged. This section traces the key developments from a simple sphere to the complex quantum mechanical model we use today. We also explore the concepts of isotopes and relative atomic mass.

Atomic Models Timeline

Dalton (1803): Tiny, indivisible spheres.
Thomson (1897): "Plum pudding" model. Positive sphere with negative electrons embedded.
Rutherford (1911): Dense, positive nucleus with electrons orbiting. Mostly empty space.
Bohr (1913): Electrons in fixed energy levels or shells orbiting the nucleus.

Isotopes & Relative Atomic Mass

Isotopes are atoms of the same element (same number of protons) but with different numbers of neutrons, resulting in different mass numbers. For example, Carbon-12 (6p, 6n) and Carbon-14 (6p, 8n).

Relative Atomic Mass (Ar) is the weighted average mass of an element's isotopes relative to 1/12th the mass of a carbon-12 atom. It's calculated as: Ar = Σ (isotopic mass × % abundance) / 100.

Electron Configuration & SPDF Notation

Electrons occupy shells, which are made of subshells (s, p, d, f), which in turn contain orbitals. Each orbital can hold a maximum of 2 electrons.

  • s-subshell: 1 orbital (2 electrons)
  • p-subshell: 3 orbitals (6 electrons)
  • d-subshell: 5 orbitals (10 electrons)
  • f-subshell: 7 orbitals (14 electrons)

Example - Sodium (Na, Z=11): 1s²2s²2p⁶3s¹

Chemical Bonding

Atoms form bonds to achieve a stable electron configuration, typically a full outer shell. The type of bond formed depends on the electronegativity difference between the atoms. This section covers the main types of chemical bonds and how to represent them using Lewis dot diagrams.

Ionic Bonding

Occurs between a metal and a non-metal. Involves the complete transfer of one or more electrons from the metal to the non-metal, forming positive and negative ions which are held together by electrostatic attraction.
Electronegativity Difference: Large (> 1.8)

Covalent Bonding

Occurs between non-metal atoms. Involves the sharing of electron pairs between atoms. If sharing is equal, it's a non-polar covalent bond.
Electronegativity Difference: Small (0 - 0.4)

Polar Covalent Bonding

A type of covalent bond where electrons are shared unequally due to a significant difference in electronegativity, creating partial positive (δ+) and partial negative (δ-) charges on the atoms.
Electronegativity Difference: Intermediate (0.4 - 1.8)

Allotropes of Carbon

Allotropes are different structural forms of the same element in the same physical state. Carbon's ability to form various allotropes with vastly different properties is remarkable. This section compares the structure and properties of some of carbon's most well-known allotropes.

Diamond

Structure: Each carbon is covalently bonded to 4 others in a tetrahedral lattice.

Properties: Very hard, high melting point, does not conduct electricity.

Graphite

Structure: Each carbon is bonded to 3 others in hexagonal layers. Layers held by weak forces.

Properties: Soft, slippery, conducts electricity due to delocalised electrons.

Graphene

Structure: A single layer of graphite, one atom thick.

Properties: Extremely strong, transparent, excellent electrical conductor.

Fullerene (C₆₀)

Structure: Spherical molecule made of 60 carbon atoms arranged in hexagons and pentagons.

Properties: Light, strong, semi-conductive properties.
